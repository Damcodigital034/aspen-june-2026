Pillar 1 · Editorial
Aspen Claims Service P&C claims partner · 1,200 followers 3d · 🌐
Florida HB-837 has been covered exhaustively as tort reform — attorney fee restructuring, shorter statute of limitations, comparative negligence shifts. The legal commentary has been substantial. What's been written about less is what changes for the adjuster on the ground, two years in. Three operational shifts worth flagging — none of them about the law itself, all of them about the file standard the law now demands. Three operational shifts worth flagging: Documentation thresholds are higher, not lower. The narrower path to litigation hasn't reduced the scrutiny on file defensibility. If anything, the files that do reach disputed status now carry more weight. Photo discipline, scope detail, and contemporaneous notes matter more than they did eighteen months ago, not less. Communication timelines are tighter. The shorter notice window means delays that used to be recoverable now create coverage problems. Pre-loss documentation requests, post-loss notification windows, and proof of loss timelines all need to be tracked more tightly than the old workflow assumed. #ClaimsWithAspen

Pillar 2 · Quote
Aspen Claims Service P&C claims partner · 1,200 followers 5d · 🌐
The cycle time number on a vendor scorecard is the easiest one to celebrate. It's also the easiest one to misread. A file that closes fast and stays closed is a win. A file that closes fast and comes back two months later isn't. It's actually worse than a file that took longer the first time — because now you're paying for the same claim twice, the policyholder has lost confidence in the process, and the supplement has carrier dollars in it that the original close didn't. The most expensive claim isn't the complex one. It's the one that closes twice. What makes a file come back is almost always one of three things — and none of them are visible on a cycle time report: Photo gaps that surface during a supplement request. Scope notes that didn't capture what the contractor later found. Coverage decisions made without the documentation to defend them downstream. None of those are mysterious failures. They're failures of first-pass discipline. And they're invisible until they become expensive. If your team is measuring vendors on cycle time alone, you're seeing half the picture. #ClaimsWithAspen

Pillar 3 · Editorial
Aspen Claims Service P&C claims partner · 1,200 followers 10d · 🌐
Vendor selection is becoming a regulatory question, not just an operational one. A signal worth tracking from this year's NAIC market conduct examination data. State market conduct exams have shifted noticeably in their focus over the last two years. Claims handling — specifically, the consistency between policy language and adjuster decisions in the field — is getting more scrutiny than it has in over a decade. Several states have published findings that ladder up to the same operational concern: file documentation and adjuster decision rationale aren't keeping pace with the complexity of the products being written. The implication for carriers is meaningful, even though it's flying under the radar. Vendor selection is becoming a regulatory question, not just an operational one. The independent and third-party adjusters working a carrier's book are increasingly viewed as an extension of the carrier's compliance posture. A file that can't defend its own coverage decisions is a problem for the carrier, regardless of who wrote it. Two operational shifts worth flagging: File defensibility is now a regulatory metric, not just a quality one. The standards that matter aren't internal anymore. They're external. #ClaimsWithAspen
